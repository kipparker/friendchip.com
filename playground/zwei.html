With their hypnotic animated loops, nostalgia laden computer sounds and leftfield humour, Friendchip create deceptively simple internet art that entertains and entrances. To celebrate the successful delivery of Scene Online we asked them to build an art site based on the theme of 'birth'. The site should also capture the spirit of irreverence, beauty and creative freedom that Playground, and Scene as a whole, is all about.

Musical cubes which 'sing' when you touch them. The Rolf Harris Stylophone revisited. Freeman's catalogue figures 'making love' in a sterile Tron world. Silicon-chip-motorway haiku. The chance to build the tower block of your dreams. It's birth, but not as we know it. And the only way to understand it is to play with it. Warning: evil geniuses at work...

Scene: So how did friendchip come about?
Friendchip#1: I was doing traditional graphic design stuff for TV and advertising. I just wanted to do something a bit different and stumbled into the web thing without really having any agenda - other than to have fun with it.
Friendchip#0: The web design came about because I was doing various part-time jobs: van driving and being a chef and stuff. Web design seemed more interesting so I did an evening course and here I am. Scene: How did you get together?
Friendchip#1: Actually he's my brother-in-law. Scene: So you can't really afford to fall out over creative differences?
Friendchip#0: We'd better not do, otherwise it'll be a bit awkward sitting round the table at Christmas dinner. Scene: Why the name?
Friendchip#1: It was something we saw on the back of a Kraftwerk album and we just kind of liked it. Then last year we got asked by Kraftwerk to design their website, which was kind of weird. Scene: Your work is very deliberately lo-fi. Why?
Friendchip#1: I've got a bit of a thing about old photocopiers - my print work is kind of based around that black and white, blocky style. We took a similar approach to the internet stuff. And we didn't have a scanner at the beginning so we had to render all the images ourselves.
Friendchip#0: Also, when we started out we got given a couple of dodgy old PCs that were being thrown out of my dad's work.
Friendchip#1: We still run all our stuff on a crappy, really basic PC, so it needs to be simple. We want it to work on whatever computer you've got.
Friendchip#0: It's kind of got the feel of the Ceefax type graphics. Those guys really knew what they were doing.

Scene:Friendchip aren't your average internet site builders. Have you had any adverse reactions?
Friendchip#0: People either get our stuff or they totally hate it.
Friendchip#1: xxx [government organisation] wanted us to do some work for them, so we arranged a meeting and said to take a look at our website. A couple of days later they sent this e-mail back saying, 'Looked at your site and didn't get the right feeling. Have got to cancel meeting.' [Laughs all round].
Friendchip#0: We've got mates who are into the whole e-commerce thing and they all say, 'So what's the point of it all? Why are you doing it?' With lastminute.com and all that, the internet has suddenly become this big money-making thing. What we do has even more of a contrast with that.
Friendchip#1: They just don't get it. Straight away it's like, 'Yeah, so how do you make money out of it'.
Friendchip#0: And we're like, 'But it's art.'
Friendchip#1: Anyway, whatever anyone thinks, we're always going to end up doing something with pretty colours and funny noises.
